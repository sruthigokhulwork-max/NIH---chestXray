[ATELECTASIS]
Atelectasis refers to partial or complete collapse of lung tissue, preventing normal air exchange in the affected region. On chest X-ray, it typically appears as increased opacity with volume loss, often accompanied by shifting of the mediastinum or diaphragm toward the affected side. Common causes include airway obstruction, post-surgical complications, and prolonged immobility. Caveat: small areas of atelectasis can be subtle and may be confused with early consolidation or pleural effusion. Model limitation: AI models trained on limited data may struggle to distinguish atelectasis from overlapping structures such as the heart border.

[CARDIOMEGALY]
Cardiomegaly describes an abnormally enlarged heart silhouette on chest X-ray, typically assessed using the cardiothoracic ratio (heart width compared to chest width, with greater than 0.5 suggesting enlargement). Common causes include hypertension, valvular disease, and cardiomyopathy. Caveat: apparent cardiac enlargement can be falsely suggested by poor inspiration, patient positioning, or AP (versus PA) projection technique. Model limitation: distinguishing true cardiomegaly from technical/positional artifacts requires clinical correlation with patient history and additional imaging.

[EFFUSION]
Pleural effusion is the accumulation of excess fluid in the pleural space surrounding the lungs. On X-ray, it typically presents as blunting of the costophrenic angle or a meniscus-shaped opacity at the lung base, often with a horizontal fluid line on lateral views. Common causes include heart failure, infection, and malignancy. Caveat: small effusions may be missed on frontal-only X-rays and require lateral or decubitus views for confirmation. Model limitation: differentiating effusion from consolidation in lower lung zones can be challenging without additional clinical context.

[INFILTRATION]
Infiltration refers to the presence of abnormal substances (such as fluid, cells, or other material) within lung tissue, often appearing as hazy or patchy opacities on X-ray. It is a nonspecific finding that can indicate infection, inflammation, or fluid accumulation. Caveat: infiltration is one of the most common and least specific findings in chest radiography, frequently overlapping in appearance with early pneumonia or mild edema. Model limitation: due to its nonspecific nature, infiltration has high false-positive potential and should not be used alone for diagnostic decisions.

[MASS]
A pulmonary mass is defined as a discrete lung lesion larger than 3 centimeters in diameter, appearing as a well or poorly defined opacity on X-ray. Masses raise concern for malignancy, though benign causes (such as granulomas or abscesses) also occur. Caveat: differentiating malignant from benign masses requires follow-up imaging (CT) and often biopsy; X-ray alone is insufficient for definitive characterization. Model limitation: with limited training examples, models may have reduced sensitivity for smaller or atypically located masses.

[NODULE]
A pulmonary nodule is a discrete lung lesion smaller than 3 centimeters, often round or oval in shape. Nodules can represent benign processes (old infections, calcified granulomas) or early-stage malignancy. Caveat: most nodules detected on chest X-ray are benign, but any new or growing nodule warrants further evaluation with CT imaging. Model limitation: small nodules are among the most difficult findings for AI models to detect reliably, especially with limited training data.

[PNEUMONIA]
Pneumonia is an infection causing inflammation of the lung's air sacs, typically appearing as a localized or patchy area of consolidation on chest X-ray, often with air bronchograms. Common causes include bacterial, viral, or fungal pathogens. Caveat: chest X-ray findings can lag behind clinical symptoms, and early pneumonia may appear subtle or be missed entirely on imaging alone. Model limitation: pneumonia detection accuracy is highly sensitive to training data volume; models trained on limited or imbalanced datasets show notably reduced reliability for this label.

[PNEUMOTHORAX]
Pneumothorax is the presence of air in the pleural space, causing partial or complete lung collapse. On X-ray, it appears as a visible pleural line with absent lung markings peripheral to that line, sometimes with mediastinal shift in tension pneumothorax. Common causes include trauma, spontaneous rupture of lung blebs, or iatrogenic causes (post-procedure). Caveat: small pneumothoraces can be easily missed, particularly on supine or portable X-rays. Model limitation: rapid clinical correlation is essential, as pneumothorax can be a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention regardless of model confidence.

[CONSOLIDATION]
Consolidation refers to lung tissue filled with fluid, pus, blood, or other material instead of air, appearing as dense, well-defined opacity on X-ray, often with air bronchograms. It is commonly associated with pneumonia but can also result from pulmonary edema, hemorrhage, or malignancy. Caveat: consolidation and infiltration may overlap in description and are sometimes used interchangeably, though consolidation typically implies more complete airspace filling. Model limitation: differentiating the underlying cause of consolidation requires clinical context not available from imaging alone.

[EDEMA]
Pulmonary edema is the accumulation of fluid within lung tissue, typically due to heart failure (cardiogenic) or increased capillary permeability (non-cardiogenic). On X-ray, it presents as diffuse, bilateral hazy opacities, often with a "bat-wing" pattern radiating from the hila, and may be accompanied by enlarged heart silhouette. Caveat: edema severity on X-ray does not always correlate linearly with clinical severity, and early edema may be subtle. Model limitation: in our experiments, this label showed strong detection performance, likely due to its widespread, easily recognizable bilateral pattern.

[EMPHYSEMA]
Emphysema is a chronic lung condition characterized by destruction of alveolar walls, leading to permanently enlarged air spaces and reduced lung elasticity. On X-ray, it appears as hyperinflated lungs, flattened diaphragm, and increased retrosternal air space. It is most commonly caused by long-term smoking. Caveat: mild emphysema can be difficult to distinguish from normal aging-related lung changes. Model limitation: subtle emphysematous changes require careful comparison with prior imaging for confident detection.

[FIBROSIS]
Pulmonary fibrosis involves scarring of lung tissue, leading to stiffening and reduced lung function. On X-ray, it appears as increased reticular (net-like) markings, often most prominent in the lower lung zones, with possible volume loss over time. Causes include long-term inflammation, autoimmune disease, or environmental/occupational exposure. Caveat: early fibrosis can resemble normal interstitial markings, making it one of the more challenging findings to detect reliably on plain X-ray. Model limitation: fibrosis detection benefits significantly from CT imaging; X-ray-only models have inherent sensitivity limitations for this finding.

[PLEURAL_THICKENING]
Pleural thickening refers to abnormal thickening of the pleural lining, often resulting from prior infection, inflammation, or asbestos exposure. On X-ray, it appears as a thin, irregular density along the lung periphery. Caveat: pleural thickening can be a benign, chronic finding unrelated to active disease, requiring clinical correlation to determine significance. Model limitation: distinguishing pleural thickening from overlying soft tissue or rib shadows can be challenging without high image resolution.

[HERNIA]
A hiatal or diaphragmatic hernia involves displacement of abdominal contents (commonly the stomach) into the thoracic cavity through a defect in the diaphragm. On X-ray, it may appear as an air-fluid level or soft tissue mass near the diaphragm, sometimes with a visible bowel gas pattern in the chest. Caveat: hernias are relatively rare in this dataset, which significantly limits training examples and detection reliability. Model limitation: due to extremely limited positive examples (one of the rarest findings in our training data), predictions for this label carry the highest uncertainty and should be interpreted with particular caution.
